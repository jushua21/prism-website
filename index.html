Quick Facts

Facts

What is teenage pregnancy?

Teenage pregnancy happens when a girl between 13–19 years old becomes pregnant.

Why it matters:

At this age, the body and mind are still developing. This makes pregnancy riskier compared to adults.

Global Numbers:

  • Around 12 million teenagers give birth every year worldwide.
  • About 777,000 births per year are from girls under 15.

Philippine Situation:

  • 1 in 10 young women aged 15–19 is already a mother.
  • Teenage pregnancy rates are highest in some regions of Mindanao, including Zamboanga Peninsula.
  • Teenage mothers are more likely to drop out of school and experience health problems.

Fact Check: Teenage pregnancy is preventable — but only with correct knowledge and responsible actions.

Understand the Problem

Causes & Effects

Causes of Teenage Pregnancy

1. Unprotected Sex

  • The #1 cause. Having sex without using condoms or birth control.
  • Even if it's your first time, you can get pregnant.
  • Reasons why teens don't use protection:
    • Shyness to buy condoms
    • Myths (like "you can't get pregnant the first time")
    • Lack of sex education

2. Lack of Proper Information

  • Myths and misinformation are everywhere.
  • Example: Some believe you can't get pregnant during your period — but it's still possible.

3. Peer Pressure & Curiosity

  • Friends may pressure others into sex to "fit in."
  • Curiosity without guidance leads to unsafe decisions.

4. Family Problems

  • Broken families or lack of attention can push teens to seek love and care outside the home.

5. Media Influence

  • Movies, TV shows, and social media often show romance and sex as "normal" but rarely show the struggles of being a teenage parent.

Effects of Teenage Pregnancy

On the Teen Mother:

  • Health risks: anemia, high blood pressure, premature labor, miscarriage
  • Emotional stress, anxiety, or depression
  • Hard to finish school

On the Baby:

  • Babies are more likely to be born underweight or premature
  • Greater risk of sickness or even death in the first year

On the Teen Father:

  • May feel pressured to earn money early
  • May also drop out of school
  • Faces sudden adult responsibilities

On the Family:

  • Financial stress (hospital bills, baby's needs, daily expenses)
  • Parents may feel disappointed, leading to conflicts

Teenage pregnancy affects everyone — not just the girl but her partner, baby, and family too.

Immediate Steps

What To Do if It Happens

If you're pregnant:

1. Don't Panic

Pregnancy is life-changing, but it's not the end of your future.

2. Tell Someone You Trust

Share the news with a parent, guardian, or another trusted adult. Keeping it secret makes it harder.

3. Go for Prenatal Check-ups

  • Visit a barangay health center or doctor.
  • Regular check-ups help monitor your health and the baby's growth.
  • Health workers may give free vitamins like iron and folic acid.

4. Eat Healthy Foods

  • Eat a balanced diet: rice, vegetables, fruits, fish, eggs, meat, and milk.
  • Drink plenty of water.
  • Avoid junk food, too much soda, coffee, alcohol, and raw/undercooked food.

5. Avoid Harmful Habits

Do not smoke, drink alcohol, or use drugs. These can cause miscarriage, premature birth, or birth defects.

6. Take Care of Your Body

  • Get enough rest and sleep.
  • Do light exercise like walking.
  • Avoid lifting heavy things.
  • Maintain good hygiene to prevent infections.

7. Prepare Emotionally

  • Mood swings and stress are normal.
  • Talk to supportive friends, family, or a counselor.

8. Continue Education (if possible)

  • Some schools allow pregnant teens to continue studying.
  • Education remains important for your future and your child's future.

9. Prepare Before Birth

  • Pack hospital essentials: baby clothes, diapers, mother's clothes, IDs, medical records.
  • Discuss safe delivery options with a doctor or midwife.

If your friend is pregnant:

  • Don't spread rumors.
  • Support her by listening and accompanying her to check-ups.
  • Remind her she is not alone.

If you feel overwhelmed or hopeless:

  • Share your feelings with someone you trust.
  • Call a crisis hotline for emotional support.
  • Remember: Pregnancy does not erase your dreams — but it requires courage, planning, and support.

Local Support in Dapitan City:

  • Barangay Health Centers – Free check-ups, vitamins, counseling
  • Dapitan City Hospital / Rural Health Unit – Medical services for pregnant women
  • Guidance Office – Dapitan NHS – Academic and emotional support
  • DOH Hotline (1555) – Free advice and referrals

Stay Informed

Prevention Tips

Best Way: Wait until you are ready — focus on your goals first.

If you choose to have sex:

1. Always use condoms

Protects against both pregnancy and STIs.

2. Birth Control Options:

  • Pills – taken daily, doctor's advice needed
  • Injectables – every 3 months
  • Implants – last up to 3 years
  • IUD (Intrauterine Device) – lasts up to 10 years

3. Emergency Contraception

Pills within 3–5 days after unprotected sex (the sooner, the better).

Warning: Unprotected sex is the fastest way to get pregnant. Even once is enough.

Other Safe Practices:

  • Learn to say NO if pressured.
  • Set clear relationship boundaries.
  • Focus on studies, hobbies, and dreams.
